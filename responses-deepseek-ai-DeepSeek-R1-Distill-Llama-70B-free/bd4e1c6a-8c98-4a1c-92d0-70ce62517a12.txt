A state law later required the city to create a general plan governing land use and open-space development. The city's plan imposed new density requirements, limiting the lot to one to five single-family residences. The partnership sued, alleging a taking without just compensation under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments but lost at lower courts. The Supreme Court agreed to hear the case.

The key issue revolves around whether the city's regulation constitutes a regulatory taking under the Takings Clause. The Supreme Court has established that a regulatory taking occurs if a government action deprives a property owner of all economically viable uses of their property or significantly interferes with reasonable investment-backed expectations. However, if the property retains some economic value or use, the regulation is generally upheld as a valid exercise of police power.

In this scenario, the city's plan allows for the construction of one to five single-family residences, meaning the property retains economic value and use. Precedents like Penn Central Transportation Co. v. New York City and Yeo v. Town of Hudson support the idea that regulations reducing but not eliminating economic use do not constitute a taking. Therefore, the Supreme Court likely ruled that the partnership was not deprived of all economic use, making the regulation constitutional.
