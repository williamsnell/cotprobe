Linkage disequilibrium (LD) refers to the non-random association of alleles at different loci, often maintained by factors that prevent the breakdown of these associations. To determine which factor does NOT promote LD, let us analyze each option:

- **Option A: Asexual reproduction**  
  In asexual reproduction, offspring are genetically identical to the parent, and there is no recombination. This prevents the breakdown of allele associations, thereby maintaining or promoting LD.

- **Option B: Coadapted gene complexes**  
  These are sets of genes that function well together and are often favored by natural selection. This selection maintains the association of specific alleles, promoting LD.

- **Option C: Epistasis**  
  Epistasis occurs when the effect of one gene depends on the presence of another. This can lead to selection for specific allele combinations, maintaining LD.

- **Option D: Random mating**  
  Random mating involves the mating of individuals without regard to their genotype. While LD can exist in a randomly mating population (especially if loci are linked), random mating itself does not promote LD. Instead, it can lead to the breakdown of LD over time by allowing recombination and independent assortment of alleles.
