The rider called the woman as an adverse witness and only asked about ownership and training, not the horse's behavior. On cross-examination, the woman was asked about the horse's gentle nature and tendency not to kick unless provoked. The rider's attorney objected, and the task is to determine the rider's strongest argument against the admissibility of this testimony.

### Key Analysis of the Options:

1. **Option A: Inadmissible as hearsay evidence**  
   This is incorrect because the woman's testimony about the horse's behavior is based on her personal knowledge as the owner and trainer. It is not hearsay since she is testifying about facts within her own experience.

   This is also incorrect. The testimony about the horse's behavior is not being used to impeach the woman's credibility but rather as substantive evidence to challenge the rider's claim.

   While the testimony could be interpreted as substantive evidence to suggest the horse was provoked, this option does not address the procedural issue of whether the testimony was admissible in the first place. The stronger argument lies in the scope of cross-examination.

4. **Option D: Inadmissible because the woman's testimony contradicts the rider's account**  
   This is incorrect. Contradictory testimony is a common aspect of trials and does not render evidence inadmissible.

5. **Option E: Inadmissible because it is beyond the scope of the woman's direct testimony**  
   This is the strongest argument. The rider's direct examination only covered ownership and training, and the cross-examination question about the horse's behavior introduces a new topic not addressed in direct. Under the rules of evidence, cross-examination is generally limited to the scope of direct examination unless the court allows it. Since the horse's behavior was not relevant to ownership or training, the testimony is inadmissible on this basis.

6. **Option F: Admissible as it provides evidence of the horse's general behavior**  
   While the testimony might be relevant, it is inadmissible because it exceeds the scope of direct examination. This option ignores the procedural rules governing cross-examination.

   This is incorrect. Bias goes to the weight of the evidence, not its admissibility. The testimony is not automatically inadmissible simply because it may be biased.

8. **Option H: Admissible as it is relevant to the case**

9. **Option I: Admissible because it can refute the rider's claim**  
   This is incorrect. While the testimony could refute the rider's claim, it is inadmissible because it exceeds the scope of direct examination.
