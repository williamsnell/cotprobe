The effects of an increase in the money supply can be analyzed using the aggregate demand-aggregate supply (AD-AS) framework. In the short run, an increase in the money supply lowers interest rates, which stimulates borrowing and spending. This increases aggregate demand, leading to higher output as businesses ramp up production to meet the increased demand. Prices may also rise, but not proportionally, due to sticky prices and unused economic capacity.

In the long run, prices are fully flexible, and real variables like output and employment return to their natural levels. The increase in the money supply, being a nominal variable, leads to a proportional increase in the general price level, while output remains unchanged. Thus, the long-run effect is a higher price level with no change in output.
