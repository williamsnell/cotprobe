The neighbor testifies that the house's value exceeds $100,000, basing her testimony on an appraisal report prepared at the husband's request. The wife's attorney objects, and the issue is whether this testimony is admissible under the rules of evidence.

Key considerations include the hearsay rule and the best evidence rule. Hearsay is an out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted. Here, the neighbor's testimony about the appraisal report's contents constitutes hearsay because it relies on an out-of-court document. While the appraisal report could potentially qualify as a business record under Federal Rule of Evidence (FRE) 803(6), the neighbor is not the custodian or preparer of the report and lacks the authority to introduce it. Therefore, her testimony about the report's contents is inadmissible hearsay.

Additionally, the best evidence rule (FRE 1002) requires the original document to be presented if the contents of a writing are at issue. Since the appraisal report itself was not officially presented in court, the neighbor's testimony about its contents does not satisfy this requirement.
