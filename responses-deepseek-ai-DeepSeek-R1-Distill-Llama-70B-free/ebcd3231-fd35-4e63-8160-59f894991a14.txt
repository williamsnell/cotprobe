The gravitational force on a satellite depends on the distance \( r \) from the center of the celestial body it orbits, as described by Newton's law of gravitation: \( F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \). For the force to remain constant, \( r \) must remain constant. 

In a circular orbit, the satellite maintains a constant distance from the center of the celestial body, meaning \( r \) does not change. Therefore, the gravitational force is constant in magnitude. 

Other orbit shapes, such as elliptical, oval, spiral, parabolic, or hyperbolic, involve varying distances \( r \), which cause the gravitational force to change. Rectangular or straight-line orbits are not realistic in gravitational systems. 

Thus, the only orbit where the gravitational force is constant in magnitude is a circular orbit.
