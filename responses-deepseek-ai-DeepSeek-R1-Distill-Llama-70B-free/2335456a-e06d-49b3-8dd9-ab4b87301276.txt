The photoelectric effect is a phenomenon where light hitting a metal surface can eject electrons, but only if the light's frequency meets or exceeds a threshold frequency. This behavior cannot be explained by the wave model of light, as the wave model predicts that the energy transferred to the electrons should depend on the intensity of the light, not its frequency. Instead, the photoelectric effect is explained by the quantum model of light, where light is composed of photons—discrete particles of energy. The energy of each photon is given by \( E = h\nu \), where \( h \) is Planck's constant and \( \nu \) is the frequency of the light. If the photon's energy is at least equal to the work function (the minimum energy required to eject an electron), an electron is emitted.

The quantum model of light, which includes the idea of photons and their quantized energy, is the model most supported by the photoelectric effect. While the particle model (option F) is closely related, the quantum model (option D) is more specific and directly tied to the concept of quantized energy that explains the effect.
