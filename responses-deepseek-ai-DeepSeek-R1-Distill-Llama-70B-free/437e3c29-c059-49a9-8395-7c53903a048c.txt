The unidirectional nature of chemical transmission at a synapse is due to the structural and functional organization of neurons. Neurotransmitters are stored in synaptic vesicles located in the axonal terminals of the presynaptic neuron. When an action potential reaches the axon terminal, these vesicles release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft. The postsynaptic neuron, typically a dendrite, has receptors to bind these neurotransmitters, enabling signal transmission. 

The directionality of this process is ensured because synaptic vesicles are exclusive to the axonal end, and the postsynaptic dendrites lack the machinery to release neurotransmitters. This explains why transmission occurs only from the presynaptic axon to the postsynaptic dendrite.

Option E correctly identifies that neurotransmitters are present in the axonal end only and that there are no synaptic vesicles in the dendrites, which is the key reason for the unidirectionality of chemical transmission.
