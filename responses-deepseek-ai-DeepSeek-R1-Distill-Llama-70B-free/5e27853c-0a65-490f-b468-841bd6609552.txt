The process of neuronal communication involves both electrical and chemical signals. Within a neuron, an electrical signal, known as an action potential, travels along the axon. When this signal reaches the axon terminals, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters, which are chemical messengers stored in vesicles. These neurotransmitters are released into the synapse, the gap between neurons, and bind to receptors on the dendrites of the next neuron. This binding can either excite or inhibit the next neuron, influencing whether it fires. Importantly, neurons do not make direct physical contact; instead, communication occurs across the synapse through these chemical signals. Therefore, the most accurate description is that neurons communicate through a combination of electrical and chemical signals without physical contact.
