The question revolves around identifying the psychological concept Coach Peterson used when he sat Jake out for the first quarter of the next game as a consequence of being late to practice. This action led to Jake being on time for the rest of the season. The key here is to determine whether this action constitutes punishment, reinforcement, or another psychological concept.

### Analysis of Options:
1. **Modeling (A):** This involves imitating a behavior, which is irrelevant here since the coach is not modeling behavior but applying a consequence.
2. **Shaping (B):** This involves reinforcing parts of a behavior to build it up, which does not apply since the coach is addressing an undesired behavior (lateness).
3. **Learned Helplessness (C):** This occurs when someone feels they cannot control their environment and gives up, which is not relevant to this scenario.
4. **Positive Reinforcement (D):** This involves adding a pleasing stimulus to increase a behavior, which is the opposite of what happened here.
5. **Positive Punishment (E):** This involves adding an aversive stimulus to decrease a behavior. However, in this case, the coach removed a desirable stimulus (playing time), so this does not fit.
6. **Negative Reinforcement (F):** This involves removing an aversive stimulus to increase a behavior, which is not applicable here since the coach is addressing lateness, not encouraging a behavior.
7. **Omission Training (G):** This involves withholding a reward to eliminate an undesired behavior. Sitting Jake out removes the opportunity to play, which aligns with this concept.
8. **Extinction (H):** This occurs when a behavior stops because it is no longer reinforced. However, the coach is actively withholding reinforcement as a consequence, making this not a perfect fit.
9. **Counterconditioning (I):** This involves associating an undesired behavior with an opposing response, which is not relevant here.

### Conclusion:
The coach removed a desirable stimulus (playing time) to decrease Jake's lateness, which is most consistent with **omission training**. While negative punishment (not listed as an option) also fits, omission training is the closest match among the provided choices.
