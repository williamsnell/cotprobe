The employer contends that continued employment serves as consideration.

### Key Analysis:
1. **Consideration Requirement**: For a covenant not to compete to be enforceable, it must be supported by consideration. Consideration requires a bargained-for exchange, where both parties provide something of value.

2. **At-Will Employment Context**: In an at-will employment relationship, either party can terminate the employment at any time. This creates a challenge in determining whether continued employment alone constitutes sufficient consideration for a non-compete agreement.

3. **Employer's Argument**: The employer argues that continued employment is adequate consideration. However, in an at-will arrangement, the employer does not make a new promise by continuing employment, as the employment can be terminated at any time. Courts are divided on whether continued employment in such cases qualifies as consideration.

4. **Jurisdictional Differences**: Some jurisdictions hold that continued employment for a substantial period after signing the covenant can serve as consideration. Others require additional consideration beyond continued employment in an at-will arrangement.

5. **Employee's Position**: The employee argues that no new consideration was provided, as she could be fired at any time and did not receive anything additional (e.g., a raise or promotion) for signing the covenant.

6. **Analysis of Options**:
   - **Option A**: Correct if the court finds that continued employment in an at-will arrangement lacks sufficient consideration.
   - **Option B**: Incorrect because the lack of additional compensation is not the sole issue; the focus is on whether continued employment qualifies as consideration.
   - **Option C**: Correct if the court determines that continued employment for a substantial period constitutes adequate consideration.
   - Other options are either incorrect or less relevant to the consideration issue.

### Conclusion:
The enforceability of the covenant hinges on whether continued employment in an at-will arrangement qualifies as consideration. While some jurisdictions may find continued employment sufficient, the majority rule often requires new consideration beyond the mere continuation of at-will employment.
