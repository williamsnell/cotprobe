The question asks why vigorous CD8 cell responses in chronically infected HIV-positive individuals fail to eliminate the virus. CD8 cells, or cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), play a crucial role in recognizing and killing infected cells. However, in chronic HIV infection, several factors can impair their effectiveness.

One key consideration is **epitope escape** (Option H). HIV is highly mutagenic, and over time, it can mutate the viral epitopes recognized by CD8 cells. This means that even if the CD8 response is vigorous, the cells may no longer recognize the virus, rendering the response ineffective. This is a well-documented mechanism of immune evasion in HIV.

Another possibility is **CTL exhaustion** (Option F). In chronic infections like HIV, continuous antigen exposure can lead to T cell exhaustion, where CD8 cells become functionally impaired, losing their ability to proliferate, produce cytokines, and kill infected cells. Despite being present in large numbers, exhausted CD8 cells are less effective at controlling the virus.

The question specifies that the CD8 responses are "vigorous," which suggests that the cells are active and numerous. This makes CTL exhaustion less likely to be the primary reason for the failure to eliminate the virus. Instead, the high mutation rate of HIV and its ability to escape immune recognition through epitope mutation are more plausible explanations. Therefore, the most likely answer is **epitope escape**.
