The question involves determining the pH of a neutral saline solution after it comes into thermal equilibrium with the human body at 37°C. The ionization constant of water, \( K_w \), at this temperature is given as \( 2.42 \times 10^{-14} \, \text{mol}^2/\text{L}^2 \). 

Key points to consider:
1. A neutral saline solution (e.g., 0.9% NaCl) is initially at pH 7 at room temperature because it is composed of a strong acid (HCl) and a strong base (NaOH), which do not hydrolyze.
2. When the solution is warmed to body temperature (37°C), the increased \( K_w \) indicates that water ionizes more, producing slightly higher concentrations of \( \text{H}^+ \) and \( \text{OH}^- \).
3. For pure water at 37°C, the pH can be calculated as:
   \[
   \text{pH} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{p}K_w = \frac{1}{2} \cdot (-\log(2.42 \times 10^{-14}))
   \]
   First, calculate \( \text{p}K_w \):
   \[
   \text{p}K_w = -\log(2.42 \times 10^{-14}) = 13.6168
   \]
   Then, divide by 2 to find the pH:
   \[
   \text{pH} = \frac{13.6168}{2} \approx 6.8
   \]
4. Since the saline solution is neutral and not buffered, its pH will also adjust to match the pH of pure water at 37°C, which is approximately 6.8.

Thus, the pH of the neutral saline solution after coming into thermal equilibrium with the body is 6.8.
