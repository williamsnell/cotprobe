When a ring is cut through the bark all the way around a tree, down to the wood, it severs both the phloem and xylem. The phloem, located just beneath the bark, is responsible for transporting sugars and organic materials produced by photosynthesis from the leaves to the roots. The xylem, part of the wood, transports water and minerals from the roots to the leaves. A complete ring cut blocks both these pathways, disrupting the tree's vascular system.

This disruption leads to a cascade of effects: the leaves cannot receive water for photosynthesis, causing them to wilt and eventually die. Simultaneously, the roots are deprived of the sugars they need for energy, leading to their starvation and death. Over time, the tree loses turgidity, stomata close, photosynthesis ceases, and food reserves are exhausted, ultimately resulting in the tree's death.
