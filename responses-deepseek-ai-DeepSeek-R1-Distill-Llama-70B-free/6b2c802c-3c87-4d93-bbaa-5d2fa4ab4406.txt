Kipling uses paternalistic language, describing colonized peoples as "half-devil and half-child," to reinforce the notion of a civilizing mission. While some interpret the poem as ironic or critical of imperialism, the dominant tone suggests a genuine, albeit problematic, belief in the duty of the "white man" to bring order and progress to colonized territories. Therefore, the message aligns with the idea of a responsibility to bring modern civilization to less-developed peoples.
