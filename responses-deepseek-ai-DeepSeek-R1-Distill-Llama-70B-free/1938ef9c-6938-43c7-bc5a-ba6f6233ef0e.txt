In a perfectly competitive labor market, equilibrium occurs when the quantity of labor supplied equals the quantity demanded. In such a market, individual firms are price takers, meaning they cannot influence the market wage. Instead, they adjust their hiring based on the market wage. 

For individual firms, the demand for labor is determined by the marginal revenue product (MRP), which is downward sloping due to diminishing marginal returns. As more labor is hired, the MRP decreases, leading to a downward sloping labor demand curve. On the other hand, the supply of labor to the firm is horizontal at the market wage because firms can hire any quantity of labor at that wage without affecting it.
