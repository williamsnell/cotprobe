The seasons in Mars' southern hemisphere are more extreme due to the planet's more eccentric orbit compared to Earth. Mars' orbit is more elliptical, meaning its distance from the Sun varies significantly throughout the year. This variation leads to substantial changes in the amount of solar energy the planet receives. When the southern hemisphere is tilted towards the Sun during summer, Mars is at perihelion (closest to the Sun), making the summer much warmer. Conversely, when the southern hemisphere is tilted away during winter, Mars is at aphelion (farthest from the Sun), making the winter much colder. This exaggerated variation in solar flux due to the eccentric orbit contributes to the extreme seasons in the southern hemisphere.
