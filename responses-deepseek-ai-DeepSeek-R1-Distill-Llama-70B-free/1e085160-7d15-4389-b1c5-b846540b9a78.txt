The Hardy-Weinberg law describes the conditions under which allele and genotype frequencies in a population remain constant across generations. For this equilibrium to hold, specific assumptions must be met. These include a large population size (to avoid genetic drift), random mating (to ensure no assortative mating), no natural selection (to prevent allele frequency shifts due to fitness differences), and no gene flow (to avoid the introduction or loss of alleles from other populations).

Analyzing the options:

- **Option A**: "Genes are exchanged with other populations" refers to gene flow, which would disrupt Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium by altering allele frequencies. Therefore, this is not a condition required for the law to operate.
- **Option B**: "Population size is large" is a required condition to prevent genetic drift from affecting allele frequencies.
- **Option C**: "Natural selection does not take place" is also a required condition, as natural selection would alter allele frequencies.
- **Option D**: "Matings are random" is another required condition, as non-random mating would change genotype frequencies.

The exception is **Option A**, as gene exchange with other populations (gene flow) violates the Hardy-Weinberg assumptions.
