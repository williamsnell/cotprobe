The question revolves around whether a landowner can regain title to property taken through eminent domain when the government agency fails to use it for the intended public purpose. In this case, a state education agency acquired land through eminent domain, paid fair market value, and the landowner did not appeal. The agency held the property for 15 years without using it for educational purposes and later sold it at auction. The landowner argues that the agency only acquired a fee simple determinable, meaning the property should revert to them when the intended use ceased.

Eminent domain typically transfers fee simple absolute ownership to the government unless specific conditions are attached. Fee simple determinable, which automatically reverts property to the original owner upon a condition, is not standard in eminent domain proceedings unless explicitly stated. Here, there is no indication that the transfer included such a condition.

The landowner accepted fair market value and did not challenge the taking at the time, which generally extinguishes their rights to the property. While the agency's failure to use the land for its stated purpose might raise policy or statutory compliance issues, it does not automatically revert ownership to the original landowner. The government's ownership is not contingent on continued use for the public purpose unless specified by law or condition.
