The DSM-II, published in 1968, categorized abnormal behavior into six major categories. These categories reflected the psychoanalytic orientation of the time and included:

1. **Mental Retardation**: Now referred to as intellectual disability, this category encompassed significant limitations in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior.
2. **Organic Brain Syndromes**: These were conditions caused by physical brain damage or disease, such as injuries or infections.
3. **Psychoses Not Attributed to Physical Conditions**: This category included severe mental disorders characterized by a break from reality, such as schizophrenia, that were not caused by physical brain conditions.
4. **Neuroses**: These were less severe, non-psychotic disorders, including conditions like anxiety neurosis or depressive neurosis.
5. **Personality Disorders and Other Nonpsychotic Mental Disorders**: This category included pathological personality patterns and other non-psychotic mental disorders.
6. **Psychophysiological Disorders**: These were physical symptoms influenced by psychological factors, such as hypertension or ulcers.
