The bacterial cell wall is the basis for Gram staining because its composition, specifically the thickness of the peptidoglycan layer and the presence or absence of a lipid outer membrane, determines whether a bacterium is classified as Gram-positive or Gram-negative. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that retains the crystal violet stain, while Gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and a lipid-rich outer membrane that prevents retention of the stain. This structural difference in the cell wall is what allows Gram staining to differentiate between these two groups.
