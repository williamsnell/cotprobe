A price floor is a minimum price set above the equilibrium price, leading to several consequences. When analyzing the effects, it is important to consider both the immediate market outcomes and the broader welfare implications.

First, setting a price floor above equilibrium causes the quantity supplied to exceed the quantity demanded, resulting in a **persistent surplus** of the good. This is because producers are incentivized to supply more at the higher price, while consumers are less willing or able to buy at that price. This surplus is a direct and immediate consequence of the price floor.

Additionally, a price floor creates a **deadweight loss**, as some mutually beneficial trades that would occur at the equilibrium price are prevented. This deadweight loss reflects a reduction in total welfare because resources are misallocated due to the artificially high price.

Among the options provided, both **D: a persistent surplus of the good** and **H: an increase in deadweight loss** are correct consequences of a price floor. However, since the question asks for "a consequence" and not "the primary consequence," both could technically be correct. Nevertheless, the persistent surplus (D) is often considered the more direct and immediate effect, while the deadweight loss (H) is a secondary welfare consequence.
