To determine the total energy released during the impact of a comet of mass \( m \) with the Earth (mass \( M \), radius \( R \)) at the minimum impact speed, we analyze the energy transformations involved.

1. **Mechanical Energy Conservation**:  
   The comet starts from rest at infinity, where its total mechanical energy is zero (both kinetic energy \( KE = 0 \) and gravitational potential energy \( U = 0 \)). As it falls to the Earth's surface, the total mechanical energy remains conserved. At the surface, the kinetic energy is \( KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \), and the gravitational potential energy is \( U = -\frac{GMm}{R} \). From conservation of energy:  
   \[
   KE + U = 0 \implies \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{R}.
   \]  
   Solving for \( v^2 \), we find \( v^2 = \frac{2GM}{R} \). Substituting this back, the kinetic energy at impact is:  
   \[
   KE = \frac{1}{2}m\left(\frac{2GM}{R}\right) = \frac{GMm}{R}.
   \]

2. **Energy Released in the Impact**:  
   The total energy released during the impact includes both the kinetic energy of the comet and the magnitude of the gravitational potential energy change. The potential energy change is:  
   \[
   \Delta U = -\frac{GMm}{R} - 0 = -\frac{GMm}{R}.
   \]  
   The magnitude of the potential energy change is \( \frac{GMm}{R} \). Adding the kinetic energy, the total energy released is:  
   \[
   \text{Total Energy Released} = KE + |\Delta U| = \frac{GMm}{R} + \frac{GMm}{R} = \frac{2GMm}{R}.
   \]

3. **Matching with Options**:  
   The expression \( \frac{2GMm}{R} \) matches option **F**, which is \( m \cdot \frac{2GM}{R} \).
