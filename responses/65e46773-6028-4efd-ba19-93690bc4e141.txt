To establish negligence, the defendant must have owed a duty, breached it, and their breach must have been the proximate cause of the harm. Proximate cause includes both cause in fact (but-for causation) and legal cause, which considers foreseeability and whether the harm was a natural and probable result of the negligence.

1. **Crop-Duster's Liability**: The crop-duster sprayed a herbicide instead of a pesticide, killing the corn. This was a clear breach of duty, and the herbicide was the immediate cause of the corn's destruction. Therefore, the crop-duster is liable for the destruction of the corn.

The fire did not contribute to the loss of the corn; it only destroyed what was already dead. Thus, the construction company's negligence did not proximately cause the destruction of the corn.

3. **Foreseeability of the Fire**: While the fire itself was foreseeable as a result of the construction company's negligence, the destruction of the corn was solely due to the herbicide. The fire's destruction of the field and the dead corn does not establish liability for the loss of the corn.
