The question asks for the yield of ATP from the complete oxidation of glucose to carbon dioxide and water. To determine this, we break down the process into its main stages: glycolysis, the conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain (ETC).

1. **Glycolysis**: Occurs in the cytoplasm and produces a net gain of 2 ATP per glucose molecule.

2. **Pyruvate to Acetyl-CoA**: Each pyruvate is converted to acetyl-CoA, producing 2 NADH molecules in total (1 per pyruvate).

3. **Krebs Cycle**: Each acetyl-CoA undergoes one turn of the cycle, producing 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, and 1 ATP. Since there are two acetyl-CoA molecules, this results in 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, and 2 ATP.

4. **Electron Transport Chain (ETC)**: NADH and FADH2 donate electrons, which drive ATP synthesis. Each NADH produces approximately 3 ATP, and each FADH2 produces approximately 2 ATP. Calculating the ATP from ETC:
   - 2 NADH (from acetyl-CoA) → 6 ATP (2 × 3)
   - 6 NADH (from Krebs cycle) → 18 ATP (6 × 3)
   - 2 FADH2 (from Krebs cycle) → 4 ATP (2 × 2)
   - Total ETC contribution: 6 + 18 + 4 = 28 ATP.

Adding up all the ATP contributions:
- Glycolysis: 2 ATP
- Krebs Cycle: 2 ATP
- ETC: 28 ATP
- **Total ATP yield**: 2 + 2 + 28 = 32 ATP.

Thus, the yield of ATP from the complete oxidation of glucose is **32 ATP**.
