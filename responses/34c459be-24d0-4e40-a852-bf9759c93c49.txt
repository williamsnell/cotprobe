The problem involves determining the frequency of the $J=5$ to $J=6$ rotational transition in a diatomic molecule, given that the $J=2$ to $J=3$ transition occurs at $126.4 \mathrm{GHz}$. 

Rotational transitions in diatomic molecules follow the selection rule $\Delta J = \pm 1$, and the energy levels are given by $E_J = B J (J+1)$, where $B$ is the rotational constant. The frequency of a transition is proportional to the energy difference between the two states, $\nu = \frac{\Delta E}{h} = \frac{2B(J+1)}{h}$.

For the $J=2$ to $J=3$ transition:
\[
\nu = \frac{2B(3)}{h} = 6\frac{B}{h} = 126.4 \mathrm{GHz}.
\]
Solving for $\frac{B}{h}$:
\[
\frac{B}{h} = \frac{126.4}{6} = 21.0666667 \mathrm{GHz}.
\]

For the $J=5$ to $J=6$ transition:
\[
\nu' = \frac{2B(6)}{h} = 12\frac{B}{h} = 12 \times 21.0666667 = 252.8 \mathrm{GHz}.
\]

Thus, the frequency of the $J=5$ to $J=6$ transition is $252.8 \mathrm{GHz}$, corresponding to option **B**.
