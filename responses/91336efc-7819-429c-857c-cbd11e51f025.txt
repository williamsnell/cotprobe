Each layer faces either the nucleus or the cytoplasm, and together they form a continuous barrier. The nuclear membrane is connected to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), which in turn is connected to the plasma membrane, making it a continuous extension of the cell's membrane system. This structural continuity is important for various cellular functions.

One of the key features of the nuclear membrane is the presence of nuclear pores. These pores are large protein complexes that regulate the movement of substances between the nucleus and the cytoplasm. They allow the passage of specific molecules, such as mRNA and proteins, while preventing the free movement of others. This selective permeability is crucial for nuclear-cytoplasmic communication and maintaining the integrity of the nucleus.
