To calculate the Joule coefficient (μ), we use the relationship between the change in temperature (ΔT) and the change in pressure (ΔP) for an adiabatic process:

\[
\mu = \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta P}
\]

### Step 1: Given Data
- Initial temperature: \( T_1 = 273.15 \, \mathrm{K} \)
- Initial pressure: \( P_1 = 32 \, \mathrm{atm} \)
- Final pressure: \( P_2 = 1 \, \mathrm{atm} \)
- Change in temperature: \( \Delta T = T_1 - T_2 = 273.15 \, \mathrm{K} - 251.15 \, \mathrm{K} = -22 \, \mathrm{K} \)
- Change in pressure: \( \Delta P = P_2 - P_1 = 1 \, \mathrm{atm} - 32 \, \mathrm{atm} = -31 \, \mathrm{atm} \)

### Step 2: Calculate the Joule Coefficient
The Joule coefficient is defined as:

\[
\mu = \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta P}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\mu = \frac{-22}{-31} = \frac{22}{31} \approx 0.71 \, \mathrm{K/atm}
\]

### Step 3: Verify the Formula
For an ideal gas, the Joule coefficient is indeed given by:

\[
\mu = \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta P}
\]

This formula is valid for an adiabatic process, where no heat is exchanged with the surroundings. Thus, the calculation is consistent with the definition of the Joule coefficient.

### Conclusion
The Joule coefficient for this process is approximately \( 0.71 \, \mathrm{K/atm} \).
