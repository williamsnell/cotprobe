The question asks about the power dissipation in an ideal inductor. An ideal inductor is one with zero resistance, and its behavior is governed by the relationship \( V = L \frac{di}{dt} \), where \( V \) is the voltage across the inductor, \( L \) is its inductance, and \( \frac{di}{dt} \) is the rate of change of current. Power is defined as \( P = V \cdot I \), where \( I \) is the current. Substituting \( V = L \frac{di}{dt} \), the power becomes \( P = L \frac{di}{dt} \cdot I \).

### Key Analysis:
1. **Steady State (Current Constant):**  
   If the current \( I \) is constant, \( \frac{di}{dt} = 0 \), so \( V = 0 \), and \( P = 0 \). In this case, no power is dissipated because the current is steady.

2. **Current Changing (Steady Rate of Change):**  
   If the current is changing at a constant rate, \( \frac{di}{dt} \) is constant, and the power \( P \) becomes \( P = L (\frac{di}{dt})^2 \cdot t + L I_0 (\frac{di}{dt}) \). As time \( t \) increases, \( P \) grows without bound. This suggests that the power dissipation could be infinite.

3. **Current Changing (Variable Rate):**  
   If the current is changing in a non-steady manner, the power can vary more unpredictably, but the key point is that the power can become arbitrarily large if the current changes rapidly.

4. **Energy Storage vs. Dissipation:**  
   Inductors store energy in a magnetic field when the current changes. This energy is not dissipated as heat but is instead stored. However, the question specifically asks about power dissipation, which implies energy loss as heat.

### Conclusion:
The power dissipation in an ideal inductor can vary, but when the current changes rapidly, the power dissipation can become infinite. This is because the energy stored in the inductor can grow unbounded as the current changes infinitely quickly. Therefore, the correct answer is:
