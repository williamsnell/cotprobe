To solve the problem, we analyze the setup involving the cornea acting as a convex mirror. The cornea has a radius of curvature of 0.8 cm, so its focal length is \( f = -0.4 \, \text{cm} \) (negative because it is a convex mirror). The object, a circular disk with a diameter of 20 cm, is located 40 cm from the eye. The cornea is part of the eye, so the object distance from the cornea is \( do = 40 \, \text{cm} - 0.8 \, \text{cm} = 39.2 \, \text{cm} \).

Using the mirror equation:

\[
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{do} + \frac{1}{di},
\]

we substitute \( f = -0.4 \, \text{cm} \) and \( do = 39.2 \, \text{cm} \):

\[
\frac{1}{di} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{do} = \frac{1}{-0.4} - \frac{1}{39.2}.
\]

Calculating:

\[
\frac{1}{di} = -2.5 - 0.02551 = -2.52551,
\]

so:

\[
di = \frac{1}{-2.52551} \approx -0.3958 \, \text{cm}.
\]

This indicates that the image is located 0.3958 cm (or 3.958 mm) behind the cornea, inside the cornea.

Next, we calculate the magnification to determine the size of the image:

\[
m = -\frac{di}{do}.
\]

Substituting \( di = -0.3958 \, \text{cm} \) and \( do = 39.2 \, \text{cm} \):

\[
m = -\frac{-0.3958}{39.2} = \frac{0.3958}{39.2} \approx 0.01006.
\]

The magnification is positive, indicating that the image is upright. However, since the object is real, the image is always smaller than the object. The object is 20 cm in diameter, so the image is:

\[
\text{Image diameter} = 20 \, \text{cm} \times 0.01006 \approx 0.2012 \, \text{cm} \approx 2.012 \, \text{mm}.
\]

Rounding to two significant figures, the image is 2 mm in diameter.

Thus, the image is 4 mm inside the cornea and 2 mm in diameter, corresponding to **Option G**.
