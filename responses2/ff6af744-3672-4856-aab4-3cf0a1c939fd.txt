The capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor is given by the formula:

\[
C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}
\]

where \(A\) is the area of the plates, \(d\) is the separation between the plates, and \(\varepsilon_0\) is the permittivity of free space. For a cylindrical cable, the plates are the inner and outer surfaces, each of length \(L\) and radius \(r\). The area of each plate is the lateral surface area of a cylinder, given by:

\[
A = 2 \pi r L
\]

Substituting this into the capacitance formula, we get:

\[
C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r L)}{d}
\]

From this equation, we can analyze the effect of changing the length \(L\) of the cable while keeping the separation \(d\) constant.

1. **Initial Condition**:  
   When \(L\) is the length of the cable and \(d\) is the separation between the plates, the capacitance is:

   \[
   C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r L)}{d}
   \]

2. **Change in Length**:  
   If the length of the cable is doubled, \(L \to 2L\), while \(d\) remains constant. Substituting into the formula:

   \[
   C' = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r (2L))}{d}
   \]

   Simplifying:

   \[
   C' = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (4 \pi r L)}{d} = 2 \cdot \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r L)}{d} = 2C
   \]

   Thus, the capacitance doubles when the length of the cable is doubled.

3. **Alternative Interpretation**:  
   If the separation \(d\) is also doubled (i.e., \(d \to 2d\)), then the new capacitance becomes:

   \[
   C' = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r (2L))}{2d} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (4 \pi r L)}{2d} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (2 \pi r L)}{d} = C
   \]

   Thus, the capacitance remains unchanged.

4. **Conclusion**:  
   The question specifies that the length of the cable is doubled. If the separation \(d\) is kept constant, the capacitance doubles. Therefore, the correct answer is:

   **D) doubled**
