Charles Darwin's theory of natural selection outlines four key conditions that are essential for the process to occur. These conditions are:

1. **Variation (genetic variation)**: This refers to the differences in traits within a population, which includes both fit and unfit genes. However, only the fit genes are passed on to the next generation, while the unfit genes remain unchanged.

2. **Differential reproduction**: This condition involves the selective advantage of survival and reproductive success, which differentiates between individuals in a population.

3. **Resource competition**: This condition involves the availability of resources (e.g., food, water) that limit the survival and reproduction of individuals.

4. **Overproduction of offspring**: This term refers to the tendency of reproduction to increase the number of offspring, which can lead to a bottleneck in the population. However, this is not a condition of natural selection but rather a consequence of reproduction.

- **Option C**: "Competition for limited resources." This is a condition of natural selection, as individuals compete for resources like food and water to survive and reproduce.

- **Option D**: "Overproduction of offspring." This is not a condition of natural selection but a consequence of reproduction. Therefore, it is the exception.
