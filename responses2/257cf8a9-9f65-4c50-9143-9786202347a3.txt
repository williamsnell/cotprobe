When the Federal Reserve (FED) sells government securities via open market operations, it takes money out of the economy, specifically reducing the reserves held by banks. This reduces the money supply because banks cannot hold as much reserves and are therefore able to lend more, increasing the money supply. The money multiplier, which is the inverse of the reserve ratio, is 20 in this case (since the reserve requirement is 5%). 

The formula for the money multiplier is:

\[
\text{Money Multiplier} = \frac{1}{\text{Reserve Requirement}}
\]

Given a reserve requirement of 5%, the money multiplier is:

\[
\text{Money Multiplier} = \frac{1}{0.05} = 20
\]

When the FED sells $10 million in government securities, the change in reserves is:

\[
\Delta R = -\$10 \text{ million}
\]

The change in the money supply (\( \Delta LM \)) is calculated as:

\[
\Delta LM = \text{Money Multiplier} \times \Delta R
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta LM = 20 \times (-\$10 \text{ million}) = -\$200 \text{ million}
\]

This indicates that the money supply decreases by $200 million. Among the options provided, the only one that reflects a decrease in the money supply is **A: decrease by $200 million**.
